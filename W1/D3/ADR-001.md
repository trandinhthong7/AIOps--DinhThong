# ADR-001: Use Kafka Between OpenTelemetry Collectors and Streaming Processing

## Status

Accepted (2026-06-03)

## Context

The payment service anomaly-detection architecture receives metrics, logs, and traces from many services through OpenTelemetry Collectors. At small scale this is only 10 services, about 50 GB logs/day, and 100K metric events/sec. At medium scale it becomes 100 services, 500 GB logs/day, and 1M metric events/sec. At large scale it can reach 1000 services, 5 TB logs/day, and 10M metric events/sec.

If collectors write directly to VictoriaMetrics, Elasticsearch, Jaeger, and the ML feature pipeline, each backend becomes part of the ingest path. A slow Elasticsearch shard, a restart of Flink, or a trace backend outage can force collectors to drop telemetry. That is acceptable for low-value debug logs, but not for incident investigation and model training data. We also want multiple independent consumers: hot log indexing, cold S3 archival, anomaly feature extraction, and ad-hoc replay after a bug in the feature job.

## Decision

Place Kafka as the transport layer between OpenTelemetry Collectors and downstream processing/storage. Use separate topics for metrics, logs, traces, and derived features. OpenTelemetry Collectors publish batches to Kafka. Flink, Elasticsearch indexers, Jaeger writers, and S3 archival jobs consume from Kafka at their own pace.

Kafka retention will be configured to 24-72 hours depending on tier. This gives the platform time to recover from backend outages or replay data after a parser or feature-extraction bug. For the Medium tier, the estimated Kafka cost in the build model is about $3,500/month inside a total self-hosted platform cost of about $13,200/month. Kafka adds roughly 5-20 ms end-to-end latency, which is acceptable for observability pipelines where alert windows are usually 30 seconds to 5 minutes.

## Consequences

Positive consequences:

- Backpressure is isolated from services. If Elasticsearch is overloaded, Kafka absorbs the burst and consumers can catch up later.
- Data can be replayed after consumer bugs. This matters for ML feature generation because bad rolling-window logic can be fixed and rerun from the same Kafka topic.
- Multiple consumers can use the same telemetry stream without adding fan-out load to every service or collector.
- Storage tiers become easier to operate: one consumer can write hot logs to Elasticsearch while another writes compacted Parquet to S3.

Negative consequences:

- Kafka increases operational complexity. The team must operate brokers, partitions, replication, retention, lag monitoring, and capacity planning.
- It adds monthly infrastructure cost. In the current estimate, Kafka contributes about $1,430/month at Small, $3,500/month at Medium, and $26,200/month at Large.
- It adds latency of about 5-20 ms compared with direct push. This is not a problem for logs/traces, but metric alerting rules must account for the extra hop.
- Incorrect partitioning can create hot partitions and consumer lag, especially for high-cardinality service or endpoint labels.

## Alternatives Considered

1. Direct push from OTel Collector to every backend. This has the lowest latency and simplest topology, but backend outages can cause dropped data. It also makes replay difficult and couples service telemetry flow to storage capacity.
2. Vector or Fluent Bit aggregator without Kafka. This reduces resource usage and can route telemetry well, but it does not provide the same durable replay or multi-consumer stream semantics.
3. Managed cloud streaming such as AWS Kinesis or Google Pub/Sub. This reduces broker operations, but it increases cloud lock-in and may cost more at sustained large scale.
4. NATS. NATS is simpler and lower latency, but durable high-volume replay is not as strong a fit as Kafka for this AIOps data layer.
